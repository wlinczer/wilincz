The Solar System

Welcome to your own solar system, the Sol System! Space is a big place and it is easy to forget just how far we need to travel just get around our home solar system. Hopefully, this page will help you understand the distances between each planet and provide some interesting facts along the way.

To begin your journey through space just scroll to the right. Just keep on scrolling and do not worry about how long this will take. This website is not as big as the actual solar system. It has been scaled down to make your space trip much more manageable. Each pixel between the planets represents 100,000 miles (160,934 km). To make sure you can still see the planets, they have only been scaled down to one hundredth of their actual size. Make sure to keep this in mind as you travel to the leading edge of our solar system.

The Sun, also known as Sol, is the center of our Solar System and the primary energy source for all life within the system. It has a diameter of 864,300 miles (1,390,473 km) and makes up for 99.86% of the solar system’s mass. It is informally classified as a yellow dwarf star but in five billion years will transform into a red giant before shifting into a white dwarf.

The smallest of the four solid planets in the solar system, Mercury consists of 70% metallic and 30% silicate materials. It has a diameter of 3031 miles (4878 km). It orbits the sun every 880 Earth Days and interestingly enough one Mercury day is 176 Earth days.

Often referred to as the “sister planet” to Earth because of its similar diameter of 7,521 miles (12,103 km) and our proximity to each other. It orbits the sun every 224.7 Earth Days and rotates every 243 days. The largest difference between Venus and Earth is its atmospheric pressure which is 92 times stronger than Earth’s.

Our home world, also known as Tera, is the only known astronomical object to permit the development of life as we know it. It orbits the sun once every 365 Earth Days and it rotates on its own axis 366 times during this period. With a diameter of 7,926 miles (12,755 km), Earth is also the densest planet in the Solar System and is the first planet to have a satellite moon which is formally known as Luna.

Known as the ‘Red Planet’ for its reddish-orange tinge, Mars is the fourth and last solid planet in our Solar System. The planet gains its reddish hue from the iron deposits which cover its surface, but Mars also has two polar icecaps of frozen water. With a diameter of 4,222 miles (6,794 km), it has an orbit of 687 Earth days and a rotation that is only 40 minutes longer than an Earth day. Mars also has the two tiny satellite moons, Phobos and Deimos.

The asteroid belt is a circular disc of asteroids and minor planets. However, the total mass of these astronomical objects is only 4% the mass of the moon. The total range of the asteroid belt is 92,955,807 miles but the majority of the belt is in between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter.

As the first and largest gas giant in the Solar System, Jupiter is only one-hundredth the mass of the sun. It is composed of primarily hydrogen and helium. Its surface layers of atmosphere often interact in violent ways and create massive storms the size of Earth. Most notably, the Great Red Spot is a distinctive storm which was discovered in the 17th century and continues to this day. Jupiter also has at least 79 satellite moons.

The second largest planet after Jupiter, Saturn is a gas giant composed of iron-nickel compounds and ammonia crystals. With a diameter of 74,900 miles (120,539 km), it orbits the sun every 29 Earth years and it has a rotation of ten and a half Earth hours. Saturn’s most distinctive feature is its concentric rings of astronomical objects. These rings include at least 82 satellite moons, one of which, Titan, is larger than Mercury and has its own atmosphere.

One of only two ice giants in our Solar System, Uranus is composed of hydrogen and helium but also contains frozen water, ammonia and methane. It has a diameter of 31,763 miles (51,117 km), an orbit of 84 Earth years, and a rotation of seventeen Earth hours. It has the coldest atmosphere in the Solar system, reaching 49K (-224C, -371F). It also has at least 27 satellite moons.

Similar to Uranus, Neptune is classified as an ice giant. It has a similar, if not slightly denser composition to Uranus as well. The planets are even similar in size with Neptune’s diameter at 30,779 miles (49,533 km). Neptune has an orbit of 165 Earth years and a rotation of 16 Earth hours. Its biggest difference is its weather systems. The fastest windspeeds in the Solar System have been recorded on Neptune with in the Great Dark Spot system at approximately 1,300mph (2,100km/h). Neptune also has 14 known satellite moons.

Welcome to the beginning of the end. The Kupier belt is the collection of asteroids, dwarf planets, and comets that orbit around the Sun but are too small to make significant note. Of course, this also includes the former planet, now dwarf planet, Pluto alongside two other dwarf planets, Huamea and Makemake. This belt extends from 2,734,033,246 miles from the Sun to the edges of the Sun’s gravitational pull nearly 2 light-years away.

The only human spacecraft to leave the Solar System have been Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 which are classified as traversing the interstellar medium. Voyager 1 will run out of power in 2025 while Voyager 2 will continue on alone.
